The Lead

Budget Season Is Over — But the Governance Failures Aren't

Every spring, school boards across the country engage in what passes for budget governance: they sit through hour-long presentations of line items, ask questions about the cost of copy paper and whether the district can switch to a cheaper internet provider, and then vote to approve a document that runs to hundreds of pages. What they rarely do is govern. And in Illinois this year, the gap between the ritual of budget approval and the substance of fiscal governance became impossible to ignore.

In at least four Illinois districts audited by the state comptroller's office this past fiscal year, boards approved budgets that bore little resemblance to actual spending — not because anyone acted improperly, but because the boards had no system for monitoring whether their financial priorities were being honored in practice. One mid-sized district in the south suburbs approved a budget that included $1.2 million earmarked for curriculum alignment work. By February, less than $180,000 had been spent on that line, while discretionary administrative spending was running 23% over projection. The board had no dashboard, no quarterly check-in protocol, and no policy that would have required the superintendent to flag the variance. They found out during the audit.

This is the central paradox of budget governance: boards have enormous formal power over district finances — they must approve the budget, authorize expenditures above certain thresholds, and accept the annual audit — but almost no structural mechanism for knowing whether their fiscal intentions are being honored between those formal votes. The budget is approved in June. The audit arrives the following year. In between, most boards are flying blind, and most don't know it. They see a monthly treasurer's report that shows total revenue and expenditure against budget, which tells them almost nothing about whether the money is being used the way the board intended.

Effective budget governance isn't about boards micromanaging every purchase order or demanding line-item approval for office supplies. It's about establishing clear fiscal priorities through the budget, then creating a monitoring system that surfaces meaningful variances in time to act on them. That means boards need to distinguish between two very different questions: "Did we spend within our total budget?" (what auditors check) and "Did we spend money in alignment with our stated priorities?" (what most boards never check at all). The districts getting this right in Illinois — and there are some — have adopted what practitioners call a "budget-as-policy" framework: the budget document is treated not as an administrative artifact but as a policy statement, and monitoring it is treated as a governance responsibility, not a staff function.

What does that look like in practice? At its core, it means the board identifies — before the budget is finalized — the three to five areas where their spending decisions are most directly connected to student outcomes. It means the board receives quarterly reports on those specific areas, not buried in a 40-page treasurer's spreadsheet, but surfaced as a governance-readable narrative with variance explanations. And it means the board's budget approval includes explicit language about its fiscal priorities, which gives the superintendent clear direction and gives the board a legitimate basis for asking hard questions when spending drifts. Illinois boards that adopted this approach after the last round of state accountability reviews have seen measurably better alignment between their stated equity priorities — the things they say in public — and where the money actually goes.

Three Things

This Week's Three Things

Q: What's the difference between a board approving a budget and actually governing the budget?
Approving a budget is a single vote; governing the budget is an ongoing process of monitoring whether spending aligns with the board's stated priorities. A board that votes yes in June and doesn't revisit fiscal alignment until the audit arrives the following spring has approved a budget but hasn't governed it. Effective budget governance requires the board to establish what it cares most about in the budget — usually connected to student outcome priorities — and then receive structured, regular reporting that surfaces meaningful variances in those areas so the board can ask questions and, if needed, direct the superintendent to correct course before the fiscal year closes.
Q: How should a board respond when the superintendent presents a proposed budget that doesn't align with the board's stated student outcome goals?
The board should decline to approve the budget as presented and ask the superintendent to revise it to reflect the board's priorities — which is exactly what a governing board is supposed to do. The key is that boards need to have articulated those outcome goals clearly enough, in advance, that the misalignment can be demonstrated concretely. If the board's goals are vague or aspirational without specifics, it becomes difficult to hold the budget accountable to them. The practical move is for boards to adopt a brief "budget guidance" resolution each winter — before budget development begins — that names the two or three priority investment areas the board expects the proposed budget to reflect. That guidance gives the superintendent direction and gives the board a clear standard to evaluate the proposal against.
Q: Should board members be reviewing the district's monthly financial reports in detail, or is that the superintendent's job?
Board members should review financial reports, but at the governance level — not the management level. That means looking for whether total spending is tracking to budget, whether any major variance categories have been flagged, and whether the district's fiscal health indicators (fund balance, cash flow, reserve ratio) are within board policy parameters. It does not mean scrutinizing individual vendor invoices or questioning why the transportation department bought a specific type of tire. The distinction matters because boards that dive into line-item detail tend to either miss the governance-level signals (the forest for the trees problem) or slide into micromanagement that undermines the superintendent's operational authority. A well-designed monthly financial report for a board should fit on one to two pages and answer one question: is the district's fiscal trajectory consistent with what the board intended when it approved the budget?
Featured District

Kestrel Valley Unified Rewrites What Budget Governance Looks Like

District Spotlight
Kestrel Valley Unified School District, IL

Four years ago, the Kestrel Valley Unified School District board received a state audit finding that noted a "lack of board-level fiscal oversight mechanisms" — polite bureaucratic language for a governance structure in which the board had essentially no way to know whether its spending decisions were being honored. The district's CFO at the time presented a monthly treasurer's report, but it was forty-three pages long, organized by fund and object code, and contained no narrative explanation of variances. Board members were either bewildered by it or ignored it. The board's response to the audit finding was initially defensive. But the board chair at the time made a decision that proved consequential: she asked a simple question at the next board retreat. "What are the three things our budget most needs to accomplish this year for students?" That question started a conversation the board hadn't had before.

What emerged from that retreat was a two-page "Board Budget Priorities" resolution, passed the following January before the district's budget development process began. It named three investment areas: early literacy supports at the K-2 level, behavioral health staffing at the secondary level, and infrastructure maintenance that had been deferred for six years. The resolution didn't specify dollar amounts — that was the superintendent's job — but it gave the administration clear guidance about where the board expected to see spending reflect priorities, and it gave the board a basis for evaluating the proposed budget when it arrived. The superintendent, initially skeptical of what felt like an unusual intrusion into her domain, later said the guidance made her job easier: she knew what she was being asked to do, and she could design the budget accordingly.

The more significant change came in how Kestrel Valley restructured its financial reporting. Working with a governance consultant and the district's finance staff, the board adopted a one-page "Budget Alignment Summary" that replaced the forty-three-page treasurer's report as the primary board-facing financial document. The summary tracks spending in the board's three priority areas against projection, shows overall fund balance against board policy minimums, and flags any variance greater than 10% in a priority area with a brief superintendent's narrative explaining the cause. The full treasurer's report is still produced — auditors need it, and staff uses it — but the board's governance conversation happens at the summary level. Board members report that they now actually read the monthly financial materials, which wasn't true before.

The outcomes over four years have been meaningful. Kestrel Valley has maintained a fund balance at or above its board-adopted 8% minimum every year since the change. Early literacy spending has been consistent with board priorities, and the district's third-grade reading proficiency rates have improved each year of the initiative. The deferred maintenance backlog — which had been growing for a decade — has been reduced by roughly 60%. None of this is solely attributable to the governance changes; the district has talented staff and a relatively stable community. But board members and administrators alike point to the cleaner governance structure as a contributing factor. When the board knows what it wants, communicates it clearly, and monitors it consistently, it's easier for staff to deliver — and easier for the board to know whether they did.

Research Note

Boards With Written Fiscal Monitoring Policies Outperform Peers on Long-Term Financial Health

A 2025 working paper from the Consortium for Educational Governance Research at the University of Illinois at Chicago examined fiscal health outcomes across 312 Illinois school districts over a ten-year period, controlling for district size, demographics, and state funding levels. The researchers were specifically interested in whether governance structure — as distinct from administrative capacity — predicted differences in long-term fiscal health. Their central finding: districts whose boards had adopted explicit written policies for budget monitoring, including defined reporting frequencies and variance thresholds, maintained healthier fund balances and experienced fewer fiscal emergencies than comparable districts without those policies, even when the researchers controlled for the quality of the district's CFO. The effect was not large in any single year, but it compounded over time — by year seven, the gap in average fund balance between the two groups was statistically significant and practically meaningful.

The researchers offer a straightforward interpretation: written monitoring policies change behavior not because they're enforced from outside but because they create shared expectations inside the organization. When a board has a policy that says "the superintendent will present a quarterly budget alignment report with narrative explanation of any variance exceeding 8% in a priority spending area," both the board and the superintendent know what's expected. The policy reduces ambiguity, which reduces the likelihood that fiscal drift goes unnoticed until it becomes a problem. Districts without such policies often have informal norms — the CFO generally presents something monthly, board members generally review it — but informal norms are fragile. They erode during leadership transitions, get deprioritized when board agendas are crowded, and can't be evaluated or improved because they were never written down.

For practitioners, the implication is clear: boards don't need sophisticated financial expertise to improve their fiscal governance. They need policy. Specifically, they need a board policy that defines what financial information the board will receive, at what intervals, in what format, and what threshold of variance will trigger a required superintendent explanation. That policy doesn't require a finance degree to write or to use. It requires the board to have a governance conversation about what it actually wants to know — which is itself a governance act that most boards have never completed. The UIC researchers note that the single most common reason districts in their sample lacked fiscal monitoring policies was not opposition or neglect, but the simple fact that no one had ever proposed one.
