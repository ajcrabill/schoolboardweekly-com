The Lead

The Superintendent Evaluation Problem Nobody Wants to Talk About

Every year, school boards across the country perform one of their most consequential governance acts — evaluating their superintendent — and a striking number of them do it badly. Not with malice, and not always with incompetence, but with a kind of institutional vagueness that renders the entire exercise nearly meaningless. The evaluation becomes a ritual rather than an accountability mechanism, a relationship-management tool dressed up in rubric language. And when that happens, both the board and the superintendent lose something important: a real opportunity to align expectations, course-correct, and build the kind of trust that only comes from honest reckoning.

In Texas, where many districts have grown accustomed to multi-year superintendent tenures — sometimes the same leader for fifteen or twenty years — the evaluation problem takes a particular shape. Boards that genuinely like their superintendent, or that fear the disruption of leadership change, tend to conduct evaluations that are more affirmation than assessment. Criteria become so broad ("demonstrates instructional leadership," "maintains community relationships") that any reasonable superintendent can point to evidence in their favor. There is no baseline. There is no target. There are no honest conversations about the gap between where the district is and where it ought to be. The superintendent walks away with a "meets expectations" rating and a raise, and a year later the board is surprised that the same problems persist.

The opposite failure is equally destructive, though less common. In some Texas districts — particularly those experiencing political turbulence or board turnover driven by ideological realignment — superintendent evaluations become weapons. New board majorities use the evaluation process to manufacture cause for termination, loading the rubric with subjective criteria around "communication style" or "board responsiveness" that can be scored low regardless of student outcomes. This is governance malpractice of a different kind. It weaponizes accountability rather than building it, and it sends a signal to every qualified superintendent candidate in the region: this board is not safe to work for.

What does a high-quality superintendent evaluation actually look like? Researchers and governance practitioners point to several common elements. First, the criteria must be tied directly to board-adopted goals — not a generic leadership competency framework, but the specific outcomes this superintendent was hired to move. Second, the process must include interim check-ins, not just an annual event; at minimum, a mid-year conversation where the board and superintendent assess trajectory together. Third, the evaluation should be separated — both in timing and in conversation — from the compensation decision, so that the superintendent isn't calculating how honest to be based on what it might cost them financially. And fourth, the board must be willing to say hard things, and the superintendent must be willing to hear them. That takes a governance culture, not just a form. Building that culture is the job boards too rarely take seriously.

Three Things

This Week's Three Things

Q: Our board evaluates the superintendent once a year. Is that enough?
For most boards, annual-only evaluation creates a dangerous lag — problems fester, surprises accumulate, and the annual event becomes either a rubber stamp or an ambush. Best practice calls for a formal mid-year check-in, structured around the same goals and criteria as the annual evaluation, so both the board and superintendent can course-correct while there's still time to matter. The annual evaluation then becomes a confirmation of a conversation already in progress, not a verdict delivered from nowhere.
Q: How do we write superintendent evaluation criteria that actually hold someone accountable?
Start with your board-adopted student outcome goals and work backward. If the board has committed to a specific literacy rate by a specific year, the evaluation criteria should ask explicitly: is the district on track, ahead, or behind, and what has the superintendent done to influence that trajectory? Criteria that are not traceable to a measurable outcome — things like "demonstrates vision" or "is visible in the community" — tend to expand to fill whatever rating the board already wants to give. Specificity is accountability; vagueness is a permission slip.
Q: A board member wants to include "responsiveness to the board" as a criterion. Is that appropriate?
It depends entirely on how it's defined. If "responsiveness" means the superintendent provides accurate, complete information in a timely way so the board can make informed decisions, that is a legitimate governance criterion worth including. If it means the superintendent agrees with the board, carries out individual board member requests, or avoids delivering unwelcome news, then it's a criterion for compliance rather than leadership — and it will undermine both the superintendent's effectiveness and the board's own governance health. Be precise about what you mean before you vote to include it.
Featured District

Palmetto Creek ISD's Evaluation Overhaul Became a Model for the Region

District Spotlight
Palmetto Creek ISD, TX

Three years ago, Palmetto Creek Independent School District was doing what most boards do: convening once a year in closed session, passing around a ten-page rubric nobody had looked at since the previous year, and emerging ninety minutes later with a rating and a salary recommendation that bore little relationship to anything the district had actually experienced. Board members privately admitted the process felt hollow. The superintendent — who had led the district for seven years — called it "the one conversation I dread most, because I never know what version of my year we're evaluating."

The shift began when a newly elected board member, a former nonprofit executive with experience in organizational performance management, proposed a governance retreat focused specifically on evaluation design. What followed was a year-long process of rebuilding the evaluation from scratch. The board started by revisiting its own adopted goals — three specific, multi-year student outcome targets — and mapping every proposed evaluation criterion back to one of those goals. Criteria that couldn't be traced to a goal were discarded. The resulting rubric had fewer items, but each one was tied to data the district already collected, and each included explicit descriptions of what "on track," "ahead," and "behind" looked like.

Palmetto Creek also restructured the timing. The formal annual evaluation moved to January — mid-year in the academic calendar — and a structured "goal-check" conversation was added in August, before school started, so the superintendent could share the prior year's final data in context. Board members reported that this change alone transformed their understanding of district performance: they stopped discovering problems at the end of the year and started engaging with them in real time. The superintendent, meanwhile, said the mid-year structure gave her something she'd never had before — a clear signal of what the board was watching and what they were concerned about, early enough to actually do something about it.

The results haven't been perfect. The board still struggles with the instinct to soften hard feedback during the evaluation itself, and one board member has repeatedly tried to reintroduce vague criteria around "leadership presence." But the architecture of the process now holds. When the board evaluates, they are evaluating against something real. And when the superintendent advocates for her own performance, she is doing so with data, not impressions. That shift — from personality to evidence — is the governance change Palmetto Creek bought with a single year of hard work on process design.

Research Note

What the Research Says: Evaluation Quality Predicts Superintendent Tenure Stability

A 2024 study published in the Journal of School Leadership — "Structured Accountability and Executive Stability in K–12 District Leadership" by Moorfield, Chen, and Okafor — examined 312 superintendent departures across six states over a ten-year period and found a striking pattern: districts with formalized, goal-linked evaluation processes experienced significantly longer superintendent tenures and fewer involuntary departures than comparable districts using informal or rubric-only evaluation methods. The effect held even after controlling for district size, urbanicity, and student demographic composition. In other words, the quality of how a board evaluates its superintendent appears to shape whether the superintendent stays — and under what circumstances they leave.

The mechanism the researchers propose is intuitive once stated: structured evaluations reduce ambiguity, and ambiguity is what makes superintendent positions feel precarious. When criteria are vague and the evaluation process is opaque, superintendents operate in a state of chronic uncertainty about what the board actually values, which makes the job harder and the political risk higher. Boards in those conditions also tend to accumulate unspoken grievances that surface suddenly and dramatically — often as a mid-contract termination or a public dispute — rather than being addressed incrementally through honest feedback. The structured evaluation, by contrast, creates a regularized channel for difficult conversations that prevents resentment from building to a breaking point.

For practicing board members, the practitioner implication is direct: investing time in evaluation design is not a compliance exercise or a human resources formality. It is a governance strategy for building the stable, trusting superintendent relationship that districts need to sustain multi-year improvement efforts. Student outcomes research consistently shows that instructional improvement takes five to seven years to produce measurable results at scale — a timeline that is impossible to hit if superintendents are cycling through every two or three years. The board's evaluation practices are, in this sense, a lever on student outcomes. Boards that treat evaluation as an afterthought are, often unknowingly, making it harder for their own goals to succeed.
