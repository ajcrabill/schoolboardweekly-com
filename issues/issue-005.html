When Board Members Have a Stake in the Outcome: Governing the Conflict-of-Interest Problem

June 30, 2026

The Lead

Florida's Reckoning With Board Member Conflicts Shows a Statewide Governance Failure

In March of this year, the Palmetto Shores Unified School District in Southwest Florida made headlines for all the wrong reasons. A sitting board member — a commercial contractor — had voted in favor of a $4.2 million facilities renovation contract that his own company was bidding on. He abstained from the final award vote, but had participated in every prior deliberation: the needs assessment, the RFP scope, the vendor shortlist. An investigation by the county ethics commission found no violation of state statute because he had technically recused himself before the binding vote. The community, and several fellow board members, were unconvinced. The episode opened a wider conversation across Florida about whether the state's conflict-of-interest framework for school board members is fit for purpose — and the answer emerging from that conversation is a qualified no.

Florida law requires school board members to disclose financial interests and abstain from votes where they have a direct, material conflict. But the statute, grounded in a 1974 ethics code that has been amended piecemeal rather than redesigned, draws the line at the vote itself. It does not prohibit board members from shaping the conditions that lead to a vote — from participating in agenda-setting, from directing staff inquiry, from speaking during public deliberation in ways that signal preferences to other board members. Governance scholars call this the "upstream problem": by the time a formal vote occurs, the substantive governance work has already happened. If a conflicted board member has had unfettered access to all of that upstream work, the recusal-at-the-vote rule provides thin protection.

The problem is especially acute in smaller districts, where the pool of qualified candidates for school board seats often includes people with professional ties to district vendors, contractors, and service providers. A district in a rural county may genuinely have no one willing to run for a board seat who lacks some financial entanglement with the local economy. That reality doesn't make conflicts acceptable — it makes the need for robust disclosure and recusal protocols more important, not less. Several Florida districts examined by this newsletter in the past year have no written conflict-of-interest policy beyond a reference to state statute. A few don't even require board members to file annual disclosure forms with the district clerk in addition to the state's financial disclosure requirement, meaning the board as a whole has no consolidated view of where conflicts might arise in a given year.

What does better practice look like? The districts doing this well tend to share a few traits. First, they require board members to submit annual written disclosures to the board chair and legal counsel at the start of each fiscal year — disclosures that are more detailed than the state requires and that are reviewed proactively when the board's agenda is being built, not reactively when a vote is already on the table. Second, they have written policies that define "conflict" more broadly than the state statute — including situations where a board member's family member benefits, or where a board member has a material organizational relationship (board membership, employment, close financial partnership) with a vendor, not just direct ownership or employment. Third, they treat abstention as a floor rather than a ceiling: board members with conflicts recuse themselves from all deliberation on an item, not just the final vote, and they leave the dais when that item is being discussed. This is a meaningful cultural norm, and it has to be actively maintained by the board chair.

Florida's Department of Education has indicated it is reviewing its model governance policies in light of recent events, and several advocacy organizations — including the Florida School Boards Association — have begun circulating draft language for more comprehensive conflict-of-interest provisions. Whether individual boards will adopt them remains to be seen. But the Palmetto Shores episode has done something useful: it has made the abstract visible. Most board members don't intend to govern corruptly. They simply haven't thought carefully about what it means to be conflicted, and no one has told them. That is, at its core, a governance design failure — and it's one that boards themselves have the authority to fix.

Three Things

This Week's Three Things

Q: Our board has a member who runs a local staffing agency that occasionally places paraprofessionals with the district. Does that create a conflict of interest?
Almost certainly yes, and the board should treat it as one regardless of whether state law requires a formal recusal. The defining question is whether the board member or their business stands to benefit materially from a board decision — and staffing placements that generate revenue for a member's firm clearly meet that threshold. The board chair and legal counsel should map which agenda items are affected (employment approvals, budget decisions touching paraprofessional staffing levels, vendor agreements), and the member should recuse from deliberation and voting on all of them. A written conflict-of-interest policy that covers family members and business entities controlled by a board member will make these expectations explicit and reduce the need for case-by-case judgment calls.
Q: How should the board chair handle a situation where a board member refuses to recuse themselves despite an obvious conflict?
The chair has several tools, and the sequence matters. Start with a private conversation — most instances of failed recusal stem from a board member genuinely not understanding the scope of their obligation, not from bad intent. If that doesn't resolve it, the chair can raise the issue at a board meeting and invite legal counsel to advise the full board on the member's obligation, which creates a public record and distributes the accountability. As a last resort, the board can vote to censure the member or refer the matter to the state ethics commission. Throughout, the chair should document every step. A board that tolerates obvious conflicts without response signals to the public — and to staff — that governance norms are negotiable, which erodes trust in all board decisions, not just the conflicted ones.
Q: Should conflict-of-interest disclosures be made public, or kept confidential within the board?
Annual disclosure statements should be treated as public records in most states, and boards that proactively post them — on their website, in their board portal — demonstrate a transparency standard that builds community confidence. The more sensitive question is how to handle a disclosure that reveals a previously unknown conflict mid-year: the board should address it at a public meeting, even if briefly, so that the recusal and its basis are part of the public record. Confidential handling of conflict disclosures, even when technically permissible, creates the impression that the board is managing ethics issues internally to protect a member. That perception is corrosive even when the underlying situation is handled correctly.
Featured District

Suncoast Ridge Schools Built a Conflict-of-Interest System Before It Needed One

District Spotlight
Suncoast Ridge Schools, FL

Suncoast Ridge Schools is a mid-sized district serving approximately 18,000 students in a fast-growing coastal county. Four years ago, the district experienced a minor but instructive conflict-of-interest situation: a board member who owned a printing business had approved a vendor list that included her firm — not knowing, she later said, that the list included vendors for a category that could reach her business. No money changed hands and no ethics violation was found, but the episode embarrassed the board and prompted a governance review. What came out of that review is now cited by Florida governance consultants as a model for smaller and mid-sized districts.

The board worked with legal counsel and a governance consultant to develop a three-layer conflict-of-interest system. The first layer is a comprehensive annual disclosure form that goes well beyond the state's requirement. Board members disclose not only their own employment and financial interests but also those of their spouses, domestic partners, and dependent children. They disclose membership on the boards of any organization that does or might do business with the district. They disclose any contractual relationship — not just employment — between themselves or a family member and any district vendor in the prior 12 months. These forms are filed in December, reviewed by the district's legal counsel in January, and posted publicly to the board's website by February 1 each year.

The second layer is a pre-meeting conflict screen. When the board chair and superintendent are building the monthly agenda, the clerk runs each action item against the disclosure database. If a potential conflict is flagged, the board member is notified in advance of the meeting, a brief advisory opinion from counsel is prepared, and the recusal — including the member's departure from the dais during deliberation — is noted in the agenda materials. This eliminates the awkward in-meeting discovery that tends to generate more community concern than the underlying conflict itself.

The third layer is ongoing education. Each new board member receives a two-hour ethics orientation in their first month, delivered by the district's legal counsel and covering not just the state statute but the board's own more demanding policy. The full board does an annual 45-minute refresher in September — timed to precede the budget season when the most vendor-related decisions cluster. Board members have reported in anonymous governance surveys that this ongoing education has made them more comfortable raising potential conflicts early rather than hoping they won't be noticed. "I used to not know what I didn't know," one board member told us. "Now I know what to look for, and I know it's okay to just say something and let counsel sort it out." That cultural shift — from avoidance to early disclosure — is arguably worth more than any policy language.

Research Note

Research Finds That Conflict-of-Interest Policies Vary Dramatically Across Boards — and That Variation Has Measurable Consequences

A 2025 study published in the Journal of Education Governance by researchers at the University of Florida's Lastinger Center examined conflict-of-interest policies across 312 Florida school districts and charter authorizers. The study found that fewer than 40 percent of traditional school districts had a written conflict-of-interest policy that exceeded the minimum required by state statute. Among those that did, the policies varied enormously in scope: some covered only direct financial interests of the board member, while others extended to family members and business associates. The most comprehensive policies — covering upstream deliberation as well as final votes, and requiring annual proactive disclosure — were associated with significantly lower rates of ethics complaints filed with the Florida Commission on Ethics over a five-year period.

The study also surfaced a counterintuitive finding: districts that had experienced at least one conflict-of-interest incident in the prior decade were more likely to have strong policies — but only if the incident had been handled publicly and transparently. Districts that had managed conflicts quietly, without public board discussion, showed no corresponding policy improvement. The researchers conclude that public accountability, not private correction, is what drives governance improvement in this domain. "The embarrassment of transparent reckoning is a feature, not a bug," the study notes. "It activates board members who would otherwise assume someone else is managing the problem."

For practitioners, the study's most actionable finding may be the simplest: the single strongest predictor of a board member complying with recusal requirements was whether they had received formal ethics training in the prior two years. Boards that provided no training saw recusal failures at three times the rate of boards that had delivered even basic orientation. This suggests that investment in board member education — not just policy adoption — is where governance improvement starts. A policy that no one has read, or that no one understands in practical terms, protects no one. The Lastinger Center has made their conflict-of-interest policy rubric available to Florida districts at no charge; several other states are adapting it for their own contexts.
