The Lead

The Board Chair's Invisible Hand — and What Happens When It Fumbles

Of all the roles in school board governance, none is more consequential and more underexamined than the board chair. Superintendents tend to dominate the governance literature. Board-superintendent relationship dynamics get their share of ink. But the chair — the person who sets the agenda, runs the meeting, shapes the culture, and in many ways determines whether a board functions as a governance body or a collection of competing voices — rarely gets the sustained analytical attention the role deserves. In Washington state, where recent board transitions in several mid-sized districts have made this gap visible, the question of chair effectiveness has become newly urgent.

The chair's power operates on two timescales. In the short term, the chair controls meeting tempo, public comment procedures, board member behavior, and the quality of discussion. In the long term, the chair shapes institutional culture — whether the board sees itself as a policy-setting body or an operational oversight committee, whether new members are acculturated toward governance norms or gradually drift toward micromanagement, and whether the superintendent relationship stays productive under pressure. A board with a weak or ineffective chair can deteriorate in measurable ways within a single year, even if the individual board members are talented and well-intentioned. The chair is, in a very real sense, the operating system through which all other board capacity runs.

What distinguishes effective chairs from ineffective ones? Governance researchers and practitioners who study high-performing boards tend to identify several consistent differentiators. Effective chairs keep the board's focus anchored to the strategic level — consistently redirecting operational questions back to staff, consistently returning agenda items to the framework of board-level outcomes rather than program-level details. They manage conflict without suppressing it, creating conditions where board members feel safe to disagree substantively on policy while maintaining working relationships. They prepare rigorously, working with the superintendent days before each meeting to ensure the agenda reflects genuine governance priorities rather than staff convenience or political pressure. And they hold a clear line between their individual member role — one vote among several — and their procedural chair role, which carries no additional decision-making authority but enormous influence over process.

Washington state has seen the costs of chair ineffectiveness play out concretely in recent cycles. In districts where chair transitions happened without deliberate succession planning, boards that were previously high-functioning became markedly less productive within months. Meeting durations stretched. Superintendent evaluations were deferred. Policy calendars collapsed. In one Puget Sound district, a newly elected chair with strong community advocacy credentials but no prior board experience turned a three-hour agenda into a five-hour ordeal across consecutive meetings, as board members relitigated settled decisions and community members cycled through public comment without clear response protocols. None of this was malicious — it was simply the absence of craft. Effective chairs have craft. That craft can be learned, but it has to be deliberately cultivated, and most governance systems provide almost no structured pathway for doing so.

The implications for board development are significant. If the chair role is this consequential, then leadership succession — identifying and preparing future chairs before the current chair's term ends — ought to be a standard governance practice. In reality, most boards do the opposite: they elect chairs reactively, based on seniority or political dynamics, and assume the role will be self-explanatory. It rarely is. The districts that consistently produce effective chairs tend to be the ones that treat the role as a distinct skill set, offer structured mentoring from outgoing chairs, and evaluate chair performance as a separate dimension of board effectiveness — not just during annual reviews, but in real time, through peer feedback and self-assessment tools that help sitting chairs identify their own blind spots before those blind spots compound into governance failures.

Three Things

This Week's Three Things

Q: How should a board chair handle a board member who repeatedly violates time limits during meetings or speaks out of turn?
The chair has both the authority and the responsibility to enforce meeting decorum consistently and without personal animus. The most effective approach is to establish clear norms in advance — ideally in a board operating procedures document — so that enforcement feels procedural rather than punitive when it occurs. In the moment, the chair should interrupt politely but firmly, restate the norm, and redirect: "Thank you — I need to hold us to our agreed time. We'll move to the next member." Consistency matters enormously: the chair who enforces norms for some members but not others invites accusations of favoritism that are far harder to recover from than the discomfort of firm, even-handed enforcement from the start.
Q: Is it appropriate for a board chair to meet privately with the superintendent between meetings to shape the agenda?
Yes — in fact, this collaboration is one of the most important levers a chair has for maintaining strategic focus. A well-run board doesn't experience its agenda as something that arrives from staff; it experiences it as a product of ongoing dialogue between the chair and superintendent about what the board most needs to govern in the current moment. The key guardrail is transparency: the full board should understand how the agenda is developed, and any individual board member's request for an agenda item should have a clear, accessible pathway. The chair-superintendent pre-meeting is a process norm, not a back channel — it works when it's visible and understood by the whole board.
Q: When the board is divided and the chair is one of the dissenting votes, how should the chair handle the post-vote period?
Once a vote is taken, the chair's primary obligation shifts immediately to implementation — regardless of how the chair voted. This is one of the places where the procedural role of chair must override the member role. An effective chair who voted against a motion will nonetheless facilitate its clear articulation, ensure staff understand the direction, and help the board communicate the decision coherently to the public. What the chair must not do is use procedural tools to relitigate a settled vote, delay implementation, or signal to the superintendent that the decision is soft. Boards that can disagree and then unify behind implementation are healthy; the chair is the single most important factor in whether that transition happens cleanly.
Featured District

Cascadia Ridge School District's Chair Succession Framework Is Changing How Boards Develop Leaders

District Spotlight
Cascadia Ridge School District, WA

Four years ago, Cascadia Ridge School District's board experienced what its current chair, Elena Morsworth, describes as "a governance reset that no one planned for." The departure of a long-serving chair mid-term — due to a family relocation — left the board without an obvious successor and forced an emergency election of a board member who, by her own subsequent admission, was completely unprepared for the procedural and cultural dimensions of the role. The next eighteen months were rocky: meetings ran long, the superintendent evaluation cycle was compressed into a rushed final quarter, and two community members filed formal complaints about the board's public comment process. None of these problems were the result of bad intentions. They were the result of a skill gap that no one had thought to close.

What Cascadia Ridge did in response sets it apart. Rather than simply electing a new chair and hoping for better results, the board commissioned an external governance review and used its findings to build what it now calls a Chair Development Pipeline — a structured, multi-year process for identifying, mentoring, and formally preparing future board chairs. Every sitting board member participates in a Chair Readiness curriculum, which covers meeting facilitation, conflict mediation, agenda architecture, the superintendent relationship, and the legal dimensions of the chair role. The current chair meets monthly with a designated "chair-in-waiting" — a board member who is being prepared for the role — and a formal shadowing protocol is in place for major governance events like superintendent evaluations and budget adoption hearings.

The results have been measurable. Over the past two years, Cascadia Ridge's average meeting duration has dropped by forty minutes. Board member satisfaction surveys — administered anonymously three times per year — show a consistent upward trend in members' sense that meetings are productive and that their time is well-used. The superintendent, Dr. James Okafor, describes the current board-chair relationship as "the most functional it's been in my nine years in this district." Perhaps most importantly, when Morsworth steps down at the end of her chair term next spring, there will be a prepared, experienced successor ready to take over — not a scramble.

The pipeline model has attracted attention from neighboring districts, and Cascadia Ridge has begun sharing its curriculum materials under an open-access framework through the Washington State School Directors' Association. Morsworth is clear that the model isn't magic: "It requires the current chair to invest real time in developing their successor, and it requires the board to treat governance capacity as something you build deliberately, not something that shows up automatically when someone gets elected." That reframe — governance capacity as a built resource, not an assumed one — is at the core of what makes Cascadia Ridge's approach worth watching.

Research Note

New Research Links Board Chair Stability to Superintendent Retention — With a Critical Caveat

A 2025 working paper from the University of Washington's Center for Education Policy and Leadership, "Leadership Continuity and the Board Chair Effect," examined superintendent retention data across 214 Washington state districts over a twelve-year period. The study found that districts with stable, experienced board chairs — defined as chairs serving two or more consecutive full terms — had superintendent retention rates approximately 23 percent higher than districts with frequent chair turnover. The effect was strongest in mid-sized districts (enrollment between 3,000 and 15,000 students) and persisted after controlling for district demographics, budget conditions, and prior superintendent tenure. The authors argue that chair stability creates a more predictable governance environment, reduces the frequency of relationship-reset costs when new chairs and superintendents establish working norms, and provides a consistent procedural anchor during periods of board membership change.

The critical caveat the researchers flag is worth dwelling on: not all chair stability is beneficial. The same analysis found that districts with very long-tenured chairs — those in the role for four or more terms — showed a different pattern: superintendent turnover increased, and board effectiveness ratings from state-level reviews declined. The authors hypothesize that extended chair tenure tends to produce governance cultures that become overly dependent on a single personality, suppress the development of governance capacity in other board members, and eventually create successor crises that destabilize the board precisely when the long-serving chair finally departs. Chair stability, in other words, is beneficial up to a point — and the governance structures that make stability valuable are the same ones that require planned transitions.

For practitioners, the implication is pointed: the goal isn't to maximize chair tenure, but to maximize the continuity of governance competence. That distinction matters for how boards approach succession planning, term limits for the chair role, and the development of chair-readiness capacity across the full board membership. A board that has invested in shared governance competence can survive and thrive through chair transitions; a board that has outsourced its governance quality to a single long-serving chair is often one transition away from crisis. The research supports what effective governance systems have long intuited: leadership development is a board-wide responsibility, not a single-seat function.
